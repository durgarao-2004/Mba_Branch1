## Section 1: What Is a Financial System?

A **financial system** is the organized network of institutions, markets, instruments, services, and intermediaries that collectively facilitate the **transfer of funds from surplus units (savers) to deficit units (borrowers)**, enabling productive investment and economic growth.

### Why Financial Systems Exist

Without a financial system:
- Bharat Chandra's savings sit idle under his mattress earning nothing
- The family that needs a home loan has no mechanism to access Bharat's savings
- Companies that need capital to expand cannot find it efficiently
- Economic growth stalls

The financial system solves the **matching problem** at scale — connecting millions of savers with millions of borrowers across time horizons, geographies, and risk preferences.

### The Three-Party Example (From Class)

```
Bharat Chandra (Saver)
  ↓ Deposits ₹20,000
HDFC Bank (Intermediary)
  ↓ Pools deposits → Lends at higher interest
Home Buyer + Small Business (Borrowers)
  ↓ Pay interest to bank
HDFC Bank
  ↓ Pays portion of interest to depositors
Bharat Chandra (Earns return on savings)
```

**Outcome:** Bharat's savings fund someone's dream home. The bank earns a spread. The economy grows. All three parties benefit.

## Section 3: Financial Institutions — Banking and Non-Banking

### Classification of Banks in India

```
BANKS IN INDIA
├── Commercial Banks (SBI, HDFC, ICICI, Axis, Federal)
├── Cooperative Banks (for society members)
├── Regional Rural Banks (RRBs) — 50+35+15 ownership
├── Foreign Banks (Citibank, HSBC, Standard Chartered)
└── Private Banks (HDFC, ICICI, Kotak)
```

### Detailed Bank Classification

**1. Commercial Banks**
- Full-service, profit-oriented institutions
- Functions: accept deposits + provide loans + payment services
- Regulated by: RBI under Banking Regulation Act
- Examples: SBI (public), HDFC Bank (private), Axis Bank, Federal Bank
- Serve all customer segments: retail, corporate, government

**2. Cooperative Banks**
- Formed on cooperative principles — owned by members
- Members are both depositors and borrowers
- Serve specific communities: farmers, traders, small businesses
- Types: Urban cooperative banks, rural cooperative banks, PACS (Primary Agricultural Credit Societies)
- Examples: Saraswat Bank, Abhyudaya Cooperative Bank

**3. Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)**
> **EXAM FORMULA: 50% Central Government + 35% Sponsor Bank + 15% State Government**

- Purpose: provide banking services to rural India, especially agriculture and small businesses
- Combine government support (public interest) with commercial bank expertise (sponsor bank)
- The sponsor bank provides operational support, training, and refinancing
- Regulated by RBI; refinanced by NABARD
- Examples: Pragathi Krishna Gramin Bank (Karnataka), Andhra Pragathi Grameena Bank

**4. Foreign Banks**
- Headquartered in one country, operating branches in other countries
- Bring international banking expertise and serve multinational corporations
- Subject to both RBI regulation and home-country regulation
- Examples: Citibank (US → operates in India), HSBC (UK → 150+ countries), Standard Chartered

**5. Private Banks**
- Majority of equity owned by private shareholders (not government)
- Generally more customer-service oriented, innovative, technology-forward
- Examples: HDFC Bank (India's largest private bank), ICICI Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank, Yes Bank

---

### Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs)

**Definition:** Financial institutions that provide banking-like services (lending, investment, leasing) but **CANNOT accept demand deposits** and are not part of the payment/settlement system.

| Feature | Commercial Bank | NBFC |
|---|---|---|
| Accept demand deposits | Yes | **No** |
| Provide loans | Yes | Yes |
| Part of payment system | Yes | No |
| CRR/SLR requirement | Yes | No |
| Regulated by | RBI (Banking Regulation Act) | RBI (RBI Act, 1934) |
| Examples | SBI, HDFC Bank | Bajaj Finance, Muthoot Finance |

**Why NBFCs matter:** They serve customers that banks avoid — rural borrowers, informal sector businesses, gold loan customers, vehicle buyers in semi-urban areas.
- **Bajaj Finance:** consumer loans at appliance stores
- **Muthoot Finance:** gold loans disbursed in 30 minutes
- **Mahindra Finance:** rural vehicle loans

### Specialized Financial Institutions (SFIs)

| Institution | Full Name | Sector Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **NABARD** | National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development | Agriculture + Rural development |
| **SIDBI** | Small Industries Development Bank of India | MSMEs (Micro, Small, Medium Enterprises) |
| **EXIM Bank** | Export-Import Bank of India | International trade finance |
| **NHB** | National Housing Bank | Housing finance |
| **MUDRA** | Micro Units Development & Refinance Agency | Micro businesses |

**Why specialization?** Commercial banks cannot adequately assess agriculture risk (weather, crop price), MSME creditworthiness (informal records), or trade finance complexity (letters of credit, forex). Specialized institutions develop deep sector expertise.

## Section 4: Financial Markets — Classification Matrix

Financial markets can be classified across **four dimensions simultaneously**.

### Dimension 1: By Organisation

| Organized Market | Unorganized Market |
|---|---|
| Regulated by SEBI/RBI | No formal regulation |
| Standardized contracts | Negotiated terms |
| Price transparency | No price transparency |
| Central clearing guarantees settlement | Direct bilateral settlement |
| Examples: NSE, BSE, MCX | Examples: local moneylenders, chit funds |

### Dimension 2: By Instrument Type

| Debt Market | Equity Market |
|---|---|
| Fixed periodic interest (coupon) | Variable dividends + capital gain |
| Investor = creditor | Investor = part-owner |
| Lower risk | Higher risk |
| Examples: bonds, debentures, G-Secs | Examples: equity shares |
| If company fails: paid before equity holders | If company fails: paid last |

### Dimension 3: By Maturity

```
FINANCIAL MARKETS BY MATURITY
├── Money Market (< 1 year)
│   ├── Treasury Bills (91, 182, 364 days)
│   ├── Commercial Paper (companies borrow short-term)
│   ├── Certificate of Deposit (bank raises short-term funds)
│   └── Call Money (overnight lending between banks)
│
└── Capital Market (≥ 1 year)
    ├── Equity Market (shares — no fixed maturity)
    ├── Long-term Bond Market (5, 10, 30-year bonds)
    └── Capital Market splits further into:
        ├── PRIMARY MARKET (first-time issue)
        └── SECONDARY MARKET (trading of issued securities)
```

### Dimension 4: By Delivery Timing

| Spot Market | Derivatives Market |
|---|---|
| Immediate delivery and payment | Future delivery/settlement |
| No price uncertainty | Locks in future price today |
| Example: buy USD at today's rate at airport | Example: MCX gold futures for 3-month delivery |

## Section 5: Primary Market vs Secondary Market — Deep Dive

### The Key Question: Who Receives Money?

> **Primary Market: Company receives funds**
> **Secondary Market: Company receives nothing — investors trade among themselves**

### Primary Market

The primary market is where securities are issued **for the first time** by companies or governments to raise fresh capital.

**Forms of primary market issuance:**
- **IPO (Initial Public Offering):** Company offers shares to the public for the first time
- **FPO (Follow-on Public Offering):** Already-listed company issues additional shares
- **Rights Issue:** Existing shareholders offered additional shares at a discount
- **Private Placement:** Shares/bonds issued to select institutional investors privately

**IPO Process:**
```
Company files DRHP with SEBI
         ↓
SEBI reviews and approves
         ↓
Price band announced (e.g., ₹72–₹76 per share — Zomato IPO)
         ↓
IPO open for 3 days → Investors subscribe
         ↓
Allotment of shares
         ↓
Company receives ₹X crore in fresh capital
         ↓
Shares listed on NSE/BSE → Secondary market begins
```

### Secondary Market

The secondary market is where **already-issued securities are traded between investors** on stock exchanges (NSE, BSE).

- Company is NOT involved in secondary market trades
- Every NSE transaction is between two investors (one buyer, one seller)
- Company's role is over — it already raised money in the primary market

**Why does the secondary market matter?**
Without a secondary market, nobody would invest in the primary market — because you could never exit your investment. The secondary market provides **liquidity** that makes primary market investment attractive.

### Primary vs Secondary: Quick Comparison

| Dimension | Primary Market | Secondary Market |
|---|---|---|
| Who issues? | Company/Government issues securities | Investors trade among themselves |
| Who receives money? | Issuing company receives proceeds | Selling investor receives money |
| Purpose | Raise fresh capital | Provide liquidity to investors |
| Example | Zomato IPO (July 2021) | Zomato shares traded on NSE daily |
| Intermediaries | Investment banks, underwriters | Brokers, depositories (NSDL/CDSL) |

## Section 6: Derivatives — Forwards vs Futures

### The Problem That Derivatives Solve

**Scenario:** You run a bakery and use 10 tonnes of wheat monthly. Wheat price today: ₹2,000/tonne. You worry that in 3 months, wheat could cost ₹3,000/tonne — wiping out your margins. What can you do? Lock in today's price for future delivery using a derivative.

### Three Ways to Trade

| | Spot | Forward | Futures |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Delivery** | Immediate | Future date | Future date |
| **Price** | Today's market price | Pre-agreed price | Pre-agreed (standardized) |
| **Traded on** | Exchange or OTC | OTC (directly between parties) | Organized exchange (MCX, NSE) |
| **Standardized?** | N/A | No — fully customizable | Yes — fixed lot size, dates |
| **Counterparty risk** | Low (immediate) | **High** (other party may default) | **None** (exchange clearing corp guarantees) |
| **Example** | Buy dollars at airport today | Wheat farmer-baker private deal | MCX gold futures contract |

### Forwards in Practice
- Two companies agree: "I'll sell you 1,000 barrels of crude oil in 3 months at ₹6,500/barrel"
- No exchange involved — purely between buyer and seller
- If the seller defaults (can't deliver), the buyer has limited legal recourse
- Advantage: completely customized (quantity, date, settlement terms)

### Futures in Practice
- MCX lists gold futures: standard lot = 1 kg, fixed maturity dates, exchange-determined settlement
- MCX Clearing Corporation acts as counterparty to BOTH buyer and seller
- Even if one side defaults, MCX CC guarantees the other side gets paid/delivered
- Advantage: zero counterparty risk, price transparency, liquidity

## Section 7: Financial Services

### Three Types of Financial Services

**1. Fund-Based Services** — the financial firm deploys its own capital
| Service | What It Does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Loans | Lend money to borrowers | HDFC home loan at 8.5% |
| Hire Purchase | Customer buys goods on instalments; financier holds title | Buying a car via Sundaram Finance |
| Leasing | Customer uses asset owned by financier; pays lease rent | Company leases trucks instead of buying |
| Factoring | Firm sells its receivables at discount for immediate cash | SME sells invoice to factor, gets 90% immediately |

**2. Fee-Based Services** — the financial firm earns a fee/commission, not deploying capital
| Service | What It Does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Stock Broking | Execute buy/sell orders on behalf of clients | Zerodha, Angel One, ICICI Direct |
| Portfolio Management | Manage client's investment portfolio for fee | PMS services for HNIs |
| Investment Banking | Advise on IPOs, M&A, restructuring | Kotak IB, Morgan Stanley advising Zomato IPO |
| Credit Rating | Assess creditworthiness of debt instruments | CRISIL, ICRA rating a company's bond |

**3. Custodial Services** — the financial firm holds and administers assets
| Service | What It Does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Demat Account | Hold shares in electronic form | Your shares held by NSDL/CDSL |
| Custodian Bank | Hold securities portfolio of funds/FIIs | Deutsche Bank holding Vanguard's India portfolio |

## Section 8: Financial Intermediaries

### Who Are Financial Intermediaries?

Any firm that stands **between savers and borrowers** performing:
1. **Maturity transformation** — convert short-term deposits into long-term loans
2. **Risk pooling** — spread individual risks across a large pool
3. **Information processing** — evaluate creditworthiness that individuals cannot

### Major Financial Intermediaries

**Commercial Banks**
- Pool retail deposits → lend to individuals, businesses, governments
- Transform short-term deposits (3-month FDs) into long-term loans (20-year home loans)
- Manage credit risk that individual depositors cannot

**Mutual Funds**
```
Investor 1 contributes ₹5,000
Investor 2 contributes ₹10,000
...
10,000 investors contribute total ₹100 crore
          ↓
Fund Manager invests in 60 stocks + 15 bonds + 5 commodities
          ↓
Portfolio earns 14% return
          ↓
Returns distributed to all investors proportionally
(After deducting ~1% expense ratio)
```
**Why mutual funds work:** Small investors get access to diversified portfolios, professional management, and institutional pricing — all through pooling.

**Insurance Companies**
- Collect premiums from thousands of policyholders
- Invest the premium corpus (primarily in government bonds and equities)
- Pay claims from the corpus when insured events occur
- Law of large numbers: not all policyholders have claims simultaneously → pricing is actuarially sound

**NBFCs (Bajaj Finance, Muthoot, Mahindra Finance)**
- Serve customer segments banks avoid
- No demand deposit base → fund through NCDs (non-convertible debentures) and bank borrowing
- Higher interest rates than banks (compensate for higher risk)

**Investment Banks (Kotak, ICICI Securities, Morgan Stanley)**
- Don't take deposits or make loans in the traditional sense
- Earn fees for advising on capital raising, M&A, and restructuring
- Connect companies needing capital with institutional investors having capital

## Section 9: Functions of Financial Markets

### 1. Price Discovery
> "Financial markets are the world's most sophisticated continuous auction mechanism."

- Millions of buyers and sellers simultaneously bid for assets
- Price adjusts until supply equals demand
- The resulting price reflects all publicly available information
- Example: When Zomato reports strong quarterly results, buyers rush in → share price rises immediately to reflect new information

**Why it matters:** Without price discovery, how would a company know what its IPO shares are worth? How would an investor compare investment options?

### 2. Liquidity Promotion
> "The ability to convert an asset into cash quickly at a fair price — without needing to advertise or negotiate."

- NSE processes millions of trades daily → always someone to buy from or sell to
- Investors can enter or exit positions in minutes
- T+1 settlement: sell today, receive cash tomorrow

**Why it matters:** Without liquidity, investors would demand huge return premiums for the illiquidity risk. Companies' cost of capital would rise dramatically.

### 3. Reduction of Transaction Costs
- Centralized exchange eliminates cost of finding counterparty (no advertisements needed)
- Standardized contracts eliminate cost of negotiating terms
- Electronic settlement eliminates physical delivery costs
- Brokerage is a fraction of a percent vs. significant cost in bilateral private markets

### 4. Diversification and Risk Management
> "Don't put all your eggs in one basket — and the baskets should move independently."

```
Portfolio Example:
50% Equity (returns: high, volatile)
30% Debt/Bonds (returns: moderate, stable)
20% Gold/Commodities (returns: inversely correlated to equity)

During market crash: Equity −20%, Gold +18%, Debt +2%
Portfolio net loss: only −5% (not −20%)
```

- Derivatives (futures, options) enable hedging specific risks
- A jeweller buys gold futures to lock in gold price for next 6 months
- An importer buys USD futures to eliminate foreign exchange risk

### 5. Signaling
- Institutional investors (mutual funds, FIIs) have research teams, management access, advanced models
- Their buying/selling activity is observable — and signals their conclusions to the market
- If a respected fund aggressively buys a mid-cap stock, it signals undervaluation
- Market prices themselves signal: rising gold = inflation/geopolitical fear; rising bond yields = rate expectations

> **Important distinction:** Signaling uses publicly observable trading of publicly available information. Insider trading uses non-public material information — that is illegal under SEBI regulations.

## Section 10: Regulators in the Indian Financial System

| Regulator | Full Name | Domain | Key Powers |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RBI** | Reserve Bank of India | Banking system | Sets repo rate, CRR, SLR; supervises all banks; issues bank licences |
| **SEBI** | Securities & Exchange Board of India | Securities markets | Regulates NSE, BSE, mutual funds, FIIs, brokers; prevents insider trading |
| **IRDAI** | Insurance Regulatory & Development Authority | Insurance sector | Regulates LIC, private insurers; approves products; sets solvency norms |
| **PFRDA** | Pension Fund Regulatory & Development Authority | Pension sector | Regulates NPS (National Pension System) |
| **IBBI** | Insolvency & Bankruptcy Board of India | Insolvency resolution | Governs Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) process |

### Why Multiple Regulators?
Each financial sector has distinct products, risks, and market failures:
- **Banking:** Systemic risk (bank run can collapse entire economy) → RBI's priority is financial stability
- **Securities:** Information asymmetry and market manipulation → SEBI's priority is investor protection and market integrity
- **Insurance:** Solvency risk (insurer unable to pay claims) → IRDAI's priority is policyholder protection

## Placement & Interview Questions with Model Answers

### Q1: "Explain how the Indian financial system supports economic growth."
**Model Answer:** The financial system connects savings to productive investment. When households save and deposit in banks, banks lend to businesses for expansion — converting idle funds into working capital and infrastructure. Capital markets allow growing companies to raise equity and debt from millions of small investors — the Zomato IPO raised ₹9,375 crore from retail and institutional investors who individually could never have funded a national food delivery platform. Insurance companies pool premium income and invest in long-term government bonds, providing stable long-term capital for infrastructure. Mutual funds democratize capital market participation for small investors. Each channel amplifies the economy's capital formation capacity. The key mechanism: without the financial system's ability to pool, price, and allocate capital efficiently, investments that generate employment and GDP growth simply do not happen at scale.

### Q2: "What is the difference between primary and secondary markets?"
**Model Answer:** The primary market is where securities — shares or bonds — are issued for the first time by a company or government to raise fresh capital. The key feature: the issuing entity receives the money. Example: In Zomato's IPO, Zomato received ₹9,375 crore directly from investors. The secondary market is where already-issued securities are traded between investors on organized exchanges like NSE or BSE. The issuing company receives nothing from secondary market trades — it's investors buying from and selling to each other. The two markets are interdependent: without secondary market liquidity, no rational investor would participate in the primary market, because they couldn't exit. The secondary market is what makes the primary market viable.

### Q3: "Explain diversification as a risk management strategy in financial markets."
**Model Answer:** Diversification is the strategy of spreading investments across multiple asset classes that do not move in perfect correlation — so that a loss in one is offset by stability or gain in another. The mathematical principle: portfolio risk is less than the weighted average of individual asset risks when assets have imperfect correlation. Practically: a portfolio with 60% equity, 20% bonds, and 20% gold performs better through market cycles than a 100% equity portfolio. When geopolitical tensions (Russia-Ukraine, US-China) cause equity markets to fall, investors rush to safe-haven assets like gold — so gold rises. The debt portion provides stable interest income. An equity-only investor suffers the full downside; a diversified investor suffers a fraction. Derivatives add another layer: a jeweller can buy MCX gold futures to lock in gold prices for 6 months, eliminating the price risk from raw material cost while maintaining profit margins.

### Q4: "What is the role of SEBI in the Indian capital market?"
**Model Answer:** SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) is the statutory regulator of India's capital markets. Its three core functions: investor protection (ensuring that companies disclose accurate and complete information before and after listing, preventing misleading statements), market development (creating regulations that make Indian capital markets attractive to domestic and foreign investors, expanding participation), and regulation (registering and monitoring all market intermediaries — stock brokers, merchant bankers, mutual funds, FIIs; enforcing against insider trading, market manipulation, front-running, and fraudulent trading practices). SEBI's power matters because capital markets are inherently prone to information asymmetry — companies know far more about their own business than investors do. SEBI mandates standardized disclosure to level this information playing field. Without SEBI, confidence in the capital market would collapse, retail investors would exit, and companies would lose access to low-cost equity capital.

### Q5: "How do mutual funds democratize wealth creation for retail investors?"
**Model Answer:** A retail investor with ₹5,000 per month cannot build a meaningfully diversified portfolio — minimum lot sizes, brokerage, and monitoring costs make it impractical to buy and manage 50 different stocks individually. A mutual fund solves this: it pools ₹5,000 from 1 lakh investors into a ₹50 crore corpus, which a professional fund manager invests across 50–80 securities, achieving diversification at an expense ratio of just 0.5–1.5% annually. The individual investor gets: (1) professional management, (2) diversification across sectors and asset classes, (3) access to securities they couldn't afford individually, (4) regulatory protection (SEBI mandates daily NAV disclosure, portfolio disclosure, and independent trustees), and (5) liquidity (open-ended funds can be redeemed any business day). SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) allows ₹500/month investors to participate in equity growth — making mutual funds the primary vehicle for retail wealth creation in India.

## Common Student Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|---|---|
| Saying NBFC is a type of bank | NBFC is explicitly NOT a bank — it cannot accept demand deposits |
| Confusing primary market with organized market | Primary/Secondary is about NEW vs EXISTING issue. Organized/OTC is about regulatory structure |
| Saying company gets money when shares trade on BSE | Company ONLY gets money in the primary market (IPO/FPO). Secondary market = investor to investor |
| Confusing forward and futures | Forward = OTC, customizable, counterparty risk. Futures = exchange, standardized, no counterparty risk |
| Writing that money market = stock market | Money market = short-term debt < 1 year. Stock market is equity capital market |
| Saying RBI regulates SEBI | RBI and SEBI are coordinate regulators. RBI regulates banks; SEBI regulates capital markets. Neither governs the other |
| Confusing debt market with unorganized credit | Debt market = organized market for bonds/debentures. Unorganized credit = informal moneylenders |
